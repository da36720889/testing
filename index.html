About

The ABOIL research group at NTU focuses on the development and exploration of non-invasive three-dimensional (3D) optical imaging techniques for biomedical applications. In recent years, we have also extended these technologies to industrial inspection, such as defect detection in (opto)electronic semiconductor chips, wafers, and packaging.

Our research team covers a wide range of topics, including optics and optomechanical design, development of high-performance imaging acquisition/processing engine, graphical user interface design, machine vision image pro cessing and feature enhancement , high-frequency signal analysis, image feature extraction based on machine lear ning or artificial intelligence, embedded processor development, and the development of heterogeneous computing architectures based on edge computing and AI modules

Principal Investigator

Prof. Hsiang-Chieh Lee 李翔傑

Autobiography:

Dr. Hsiang-Chieh Lee is currently an Associate Professor at the Graduate Institute of Photonics and Optoelectronics (GIPO) and the Department of Electrical Engineering at National Taiwan University (NTU). He received his Bachelor's and Master's degrees in Civil Engineering and Electro-Optical Engineering from NTU in 2004 and 2006, respectively. He then pursued a Ph.D. in Electrical Engineering at the Department of Electrical Engineering and Computer Science (EECS) at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), focusing on the bioelectronics division, and obtained his doctorate in 2017. Dr. Lee joined NTU as an Assistant Professor in February 2017 and founded the Advanced Biomedical Optical Imaging Laboratory (ABOIL)at GIPO.

Dr. Lee is a member of the Phi Tau Phi Scholastic Honor Society of the Republic of China (R.O.C.) and a recipient of both the Young Scholar Fellowship Program and the Career Development Grant awarded by the Ministry of Science and Technology (MOST, now the National Science and Technology Council, NSTC) and the National Health Research Institutes (NHRI) in Taiwan, respectively. In 2023, he was also honored with the NSTC Ta-You Wu Memorial Award in recognition of his contributions to the field of optics and photonics.

Dr. Lee's current research interests include the development of optical coherence tomography (OCT) and functional OCT imaging technologies for a variety of biomedical applications, such as endoscopy, dentistry, dermatology, oncology, and ophthalmology. In collaboration with the laser industry, he is also working on the development of novel laser light sources for OCT and nondestructive inspection or testing applications. In addition, Dr. Lee has been developing machine learning and artificial intelligence (AI)-based image processing algorithms for biomedical and industrial nondestructive inspection. Recently, his group has focused on creating low-cost yet intelligent imaging instruments that leverage edge computing and heterogeneous computation frameworks.

Dr. Lee has delivered invited talks at various international conferences, including SPIE BISC and OPIC ALPS in Japan. He also serves as a member of the Technical Program Committee (TPC) for SPIE BISC, OPIC ALPS, and OPTICA/SPIE ECBO conferences.

High-speed optical tomographic imaging technology for industrial applications

  2. High-power femtosecond laser processing has become a powerful tool for material modification, which requires precise evaluation of laser-induced effects in biological and industrial samples. Conventional inspection methods are either destructive or have insufficient resolution, making it difficult to monitor subtle internal changes in samples. To address this limitation, we aim to develop a multimodal system that integrates femtosecond laser ablation/machining with an optical coherence tomography (OCT) system for real-time, non-invasive evaluation.

Novel optical tomographic technologies for next-generation digital dentistry applications

  1. Optical coherence tomography (OCT) enables high-resolution, non-invasive imaging of dental tissues but remains limited by artifacts such as shadowing and refractive distortions. To address these challenges, we developed a high-speed swept-source OCT system incorporating an optical clock frequency-doubling (OCFD) module and a dual-axis goniometer for multi-view imaging. A refractive distortion correction and multi-view registration algorithm, integrating surface contour extraction and Iterative Closest Point (ICP)-based alignment, was implemented. This approach effectively restored geometric accuracy, enhanced visualization of internal tooth structures, and reduced misalignment artifacts. Comparative evaluation of different fusion strategies further revealed trade-offs between edge preservation and uniform information filling, underscoring the potential of this method to improve OCT image quality for dental diagnostics.

    In parallel, we developed a novel swept-source OCT (SS-OCT) imaging system based on depth-encoded multiplexing combined with an optical frequency clock doubling circuit module. This configuration enables simultaneous acquisition of OCT images from three distinct viewing angles within a single scan, eliminating the need for mechanical sample movement. By integrating the aforementioned correction algorithm, the system compensates for pincushion distortion from optical design and geometric deformation arising from neglected refractive index effects. The ICP algorithm is then applied to align and fuse the multi-view images, thereby minimizing artifacts and improving resolution. Building on this foundation, the system is envisioned to be advanced into a handheld, miniaturized multi-view intraoral scanning probe capable of delivering real-time stitched images, thus enhancing clinical applicability and supporting more effective intraoral disease diagnosis.

Portable optical imaging platform based on an embedded system

  1. Optical coherence tomography (OCT) provides non-invasive, high-resolution imaging of biological tissues and is widely used in ophthalmology and dermatology for early diagnosis and treatment monitoring. However, conventional OCT systems remain costly and bulky, limiting accessibility in resource-constrained settings. While portable designs using single-board computers or GPU-based platforms have been explored, their restricted processing power or competition between image reconstruction and AI workloads constrain clinical utility. To overcome these challenges, we developed a compact spectral-domain OCT (SD-OCT) system that integrates FPGA-based real-time reconstruction with a Jetson platform dedicated to graphical interface and AI functions. The system leverages a Xilinx Zynq UltraScale+ MPSoC ZCU104, which combines ARM processors and FPGA fabric for MEMS scanning, timing control, and real-time reconstruction, while high-speed USB 3.0 communication is enabled through a Cypress EZ-USB FX3 for seamless data transfer to the Jetson Xavier NX. The optical engine, adapted from the Optical Skin Viewer (OPXION Technology Inc.), employs an 840 nm super-luminescent diode and a MEMS-based handheld probe, with interference signals detected by a spectrometer equipped with a Teledyne e2v line-scan camera. This architecture reduces system cost and footprint while enabling intelligent and portable OCT solutions for broader healthcare deployment.

Machine learning/AI-based imaging algorithm development

  1. Optical coherence tomography (OCT) and OCT angiography (OCTA) are indispensable retinal imaging modalities that provide detailed insights into both structural morphology and vascular networks, making them critical tools for the diagnosis and management of ophthalmic diseases. Despite their clinical value, conventional diagnostic approaches remain time-consuming and heavily reliant on the expertise of ophthalmologists, which not only introduces inter-observer variability but also limits the scalability of large population-based screening programs. In recent years, the rapid advancement of deep learning has created transformative opportunities for automated image interpretation in medical imaging, offering the potential to standardize assessments and improve efficiency. In this study, we employ a range of convolutional neural network (CNN) architectures, including VGG16, VGG19, ResNet50, DenseNet121, and EfficientNetB0, together with the increasingly prominent Transformer-based models. By integrating complementary structural and vascular information from OCT and OCTA, our framework aims to automatically classify myopic choroidal neovascularization (mCNV) into active and inactive stages, thereby enhancing diagnostic accuracy and demonstrating strong potential for medical clinical application.
